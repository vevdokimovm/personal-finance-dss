## Slide 1 — Title · ~18s · by 0:18

> Picture this: it's the end of the month and you've got thirty thousand rubles to spare. What do you do with it — pay down your mortgage early, or save for a goal? Most people don't know the answer. My name is [name], and we built FINPILOT — a financial navigator that answers that question for you.

*[Open with a pause. Deliver the question slowly — let the room try it on.]*

---

## Slide 2 — Problem · ~30s · by 0:48

> Nearly 52% of people say it outright: they know the basics, but they get lost in complex financial decisions. "Pay off debt or save" is the most common question — and there's no simple answer. So what does the market offer? Banking apps show where your money went — the past. Seventy percent of people live inside that built-in dashboard. But nobody tells them what to do next.

---

## Slide 3 — Solution · ~32s · by 1:20

> FINPILOT isn't another expense tracker. It's an advisor. It does three things. First, it tells you what to do: how much to put toward debt, reserve, and goals. Second, it explains why: there's transparent math behind it, not a black box — you see how and why it's calculated. And third, it shows the future: a forecast with a confidence interval — what happens to your finances under that plan.

---

## Slide 4 — Why now · ~22s · by 1:42

> And here's the key insight. We asked people what they trust more — a transparent algorithm, or AI as a black box. Transparency won: thirty-two percent to twenty-two. Right now everyone is building AI assistants. Our explainability is the counter-positioning. And the prescriptive layer — the part that actually advises — nobody does it yet.

---

## Slide 5 — Product · ~38s · by 2:20

> Here's what it looks like. A person enters income, expenses, debts, and goals. The engine computes the key metrics — reserve, liquidity, debt load, buffer — and returns a concrete monthly recommendation: say, twenty percent to debt, forty to reserve, forty to goals. And, crucially, an explanation of why. Under the hood: weighted optimization, the Avalanche method, and a Monte-Carlo forecast. And this is a working product, version 2.0.2 — not a mockup.

*[This is the core. Don't rush it. Gesture to the recommendation and to the "why."]*

---

## Slide 6 — Market · ~26s · by 2:46

> The market is large. The whole money-management market — around eighty million users. Our segment — the people who get lost in complex decisions — is about eight million, that's ten to thirteen billion rubles a year. Realistically, over three to five years, we're targeting an obtainable share. And we sized it bottom-up, not top-down.

## Slide 7 — Business model · ~30s · by 3:16

> How we make money. A B2C subscription, freemium: tracking is free, and the engine that advises and forecasts is paid — two to three hundred rubles a month. But honestly: the subscription is the storefront. The real money and defensibility are in B2B — we license the engine through an API to advisors, services, and banks under white-label. It's not a choice, it's a sequence: B2C gives us proof and data, B2B gives us revenue.

*[Hit "the real money is in B2B" with emphasis. That's what the investor is listening for.]*

---

## Slide 8 — Unit economics · ~22s · by 3:38

> The economics. LTV is three thousand rubles. On organic, customer acquisition is a thousand — that's LTV to CAC of three to one. And the key point: we charge two-fifty, but we save the client thousands in interest overpayment. The value is many times the price. So we grow organically, and we only turn on paid ads once retention is solid.

## Slide 9 — Competition · ~22s · by 4:00

> Who are the competitors? Bank PFM — free, but it's a blind spot: the past only. Trackers — pretty, but they describe, they don't advise. Excel — abandoned in two weeks. We're the only ones who tell you what to do, explain why, and give a forecast. That's our moat.

Second — we surveyed three hundred eighty-five people, three times more than the strongest teams in our cohort. And third — the top trust drivers people named themselves: comparing alternatives, numeric explanations, transparency — that's literally a description of FINPILOT. Demand is validated by our own research.

## Delivery notes

1. **Pace.** ~140 words per minute. Let the key numbers (51.9% · 32 vs 22 · LTV 3:1) land — pause before and after.
2. **Two anchors.** Slide 5 (product) and slide 7 ("money is in B2B") are the most important. Don't rush them, even if you're tight on time.
3. **Where to save time** if you run long: slides 6, 9, and 11 can each be trimmed by 3–5 seconds without losing the point.
4. **It's a guide, not a read-aloud.** Memorize the first line of each slide — the rest will follow.
5. **Buffer.** Aim for 5:50. The extra 10 seconds is insurance against a stumble and room to breathe.
