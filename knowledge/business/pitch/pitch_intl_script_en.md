## Slide 1 — Title · ~18s · by 0:18

> Imagine an app anyone, anywhere, could trust to tell them what to do with their money — and show its work. That's FINPILOT. My name is [name], and we're building a transparent engine for financial decisions: it tells you what to do, and shows the math. Not a black box.

*[Open with a pause. "Not a black box" — slow and deliberate.]*

---

## Slide 2 — Problem · ~30s · by 0:48

> Everywhere in the world, people face the same question: pay off debt, or save? And no app gives them a simple, trusted answer. Forty-five million Americans use a digital money tool — yet most still don't know what to do with their cash. When Mint shut down in 2024, millions were left searching. Trackers show you the past; AI apps nudge you — but as an opaque black box.

---

## Slide 3 — Why now · ~24s · by 1:12

> And here's the timing. AI money apps are the number-one trend — everyone's building an assistant. But trusting a black box with your money is hard. In our research, people trusted a transparent algorithm over black-box AI — thirty-two percent to twenty-two. Open banking — Plaid, PSD2 — already makes the data easy. The gap isn't access; it's trust. And trust is exactly what we're built on.

---

## Slide 4 — Solution · ~30s · by 1:42

> FINPILOT is a transparent advisor, not another tracker. It does three things. It tells you what to do — a concrete monthly split of your free cash across debt, reserve, and goals. It explains why — transparent math, you see how it's calculated, not a black box. And it shows the future — a forecast with a confidence interval for the plan you choose.

---

## Slide 5 — Product · ~36s · by 2:18

> Here's how it works. A person enters income, expenses, debts, and goals. The engine computes the key metrics — reserve, liquidity, debt load, buffer — and returns a concrete recommendation: say, twenty percent to debt, forty to reserve, forty to goals. And, crucially, an explanation of why. Under the hood: weighted optimization, the Avalanche method, and a Monte-Carlo forecast — all of it explainable. And this is a working product, version 2.0.2 — not a mockup.

*[The core. Don't rush. Point to the recommendation and the "why."]*

---

## Slide 6 — Market · ~26s · by 2:44

> The market is large, fast-growing, and fragmented. Global personal-finance apps are a twenty-five-to-thirty-billion-dollar market, growing around twenty percent a year. The advisory-software niche we sit in is three-to-four billion. North America is about half; Asia-Pacific is the fastest-growing. We size our obtainable share bottom-up — and we reach it through infrastructure, not a frontal consumer war.

---

## Slide 7 — Competition · ~24s · by 3:08

> Who are the competitors? After Mint closed, the field consolidated — Monarch, Rocket Money, YNAB, Cleo. But look closely: trackers describe the past; YNAB is manual budgeting with no forecast; Cleo gives advice, but as a black box. No one owns transparent, holistic, prescriptive advice. That open space is ours.

---

## Slide 8 — Our edge · ~28s · by 3:36

> And that's the wedge: explainability. For us, transparency isn't a feature — it's the strategy. It does three things. It earns trust — people believe what they can see over an opaque assistant. It unlocks compliance — banks and advisors need auditable, explainable logic, and we have it. And it turns the engine into infrastructure — licensable, not just another app. A bigger, more defensible market.

*[This is the heart of the global story. Hit "infrastructure" with weight.]*

---

## Slide 9 — Business model · ~30s · by 4:06

> So the model is infrastructure first, the app as proof. The real revenue is B2B: we license the engine through an API — to fintechs, advisors, and banks — where explainability is a compliance advantage. The consumer app is the proof — it validates the value, feeds us data, and gives us a public case. It's a sequence: prove with consumers, then license to institutions. Higher ceiling, and defensible.

---

## Slide 10 — Go-to-market · ~26s · by 4:32

> Our go-to-market avoids the incumbents. We don't fight a frontal consumer war in the US. First, we prove the model at home — Russia and the CIS, where the engine is built and the market is less crowded. Bank sync is one aggregator integration — Plaid or Tink — that reaches thousands of banks. Then we expand abroad through B2B, where explainability wins. And emerging markets, with Asia-Pacific growing fastest, are open.

---

## Slide 11 — Traction · ~24s · by 4:56

> And this isn't a hypothesis. We have a working product — a live engine, version 2.0.2. Our research shows people trust the transparent over the black box. And the core is source-agnostic by design: pluggable providers let us add new markets and B2B clients without rebuilding the engine. The architecture is ready to scale.

---

## Slide 12 — Team · ~18s · by 5:14

> About the team, honestly. Right now, it's me — founder and developer. But single-handedly, I designed and built a working product: the engine, the backend, and the interface. For an early stage, that's the strongest signal there is — execution is real, the product already exists.

*[Don't apologize for being solo. It's proof you build, not just pitch.]*

## Slide 13 — The ask · ~28s · by 5:42

The roadmap: next six months, prove at home with the bot and retention; up to eighteen months, the provider layer, the paid engine, and a first B2B pilot; beyond that, B2B infrastructure abroad — the explainability wedge.
